Okay, let's talk about the SpaceX IPO. It's everywhere. The company priced
the largest IPO in history this week — 555.6 million shares at $135, raising
$75 billion, trading on the Nasdaq as **SPCX** — Elon Musk is officially the
world's first trillionaire, and the stock has already surged to about $173 a
share.

It's an exciting story. But before you blast off and put your life savings
into it, we need to talk about some "boring" terms — the ones that separate
the pros from the dreamers.

## Market cap: the actual price tag

Most people look at the **share price** — the cost of one single stock — and
think, "hey, $173 isn't that bad!" But smart investors look at the **market
capitalization** ("market cap"): the total price of the entire business.

At $173 a share, SpaceX is sitting at a massive **$2.36 trillion**. To put
that in perspective: for the same price, you could buy all of Meta _and_ all
of Berkshire Hathaway. The story is great. The price is a doozy.

## Investing vs. speculating

There's a big difference, and it's worth being honest with yourself about
which one you're doing.

- **Investing** is looking at the numbers — **earnings per share (EPS)**,
  **free cash flow** — to see whether the business actually makes money at
  the price you're paying.
- **Speculating** is buying the story or the hype, regardless of the price.

A rule of thumb I like: _if you'd still buy the stock after adding a zero to
the price, you're speculating, not investing._

Where does SpaceX land? The S-1 shows about $18 billion in 2025 revenue and a
$4.9 billion net loss — no profit — which puts the **price-to-sales ratio**
around 126. **Blue-chip stocks** (big, stable, profitable companies) like
Google or Microsoft usually trade between 8 and 12 times sales. At 126,
you're paying a huge premium for a "maybe."

## Check your vibe: risk and time

Before buying any **equities** (just another word for stocks), check two
things about yourself:

- **Risk tolerance vs. risk capacity.** Tolerance is how much you'll freak
  out when the market gets **volatile** and prices swing. Capacity is whether
  your bank account can actually absorb the loss if things go south. They're
  not the same thing, and you need both.
- **Time horizon.** How long can you leave the money alone? For SpaceX to
  grow into its current price, it might need to expand 20x–40x — which could
  mean twelve or more years of massive, uninterrupted growth.

## The bottom line: diversify

It's easy to get complacent when everything is going up. But remember:
Amazon — _Amazon_ — dropped 95% in the dot-com crash. That's why
**diversification** is key. Don't put all your money into one rocket ship.
Spread it across **asset classes** — stocks, bonds, cash — so a **bear
market** (a drop of 20% or more) in one sector doesn't take you down with it.

SpaceX is incredible. But a great story becomes a bad investment if you pay
the wrong price. Stay smart out there.
